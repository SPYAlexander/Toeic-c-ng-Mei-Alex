Part 2 · Reading

Duyên — Fate and Its Meaning

A true story, and a Buddhist perspective on the meetings that shape our lives.

Questions 1–6 refer to the following passage.

It began in the most unremarkable way. Alex was sitting in a small coffee shop when he first noticed her — moving quickly between tables, a little clumsy, entirely unaware of the warmth she brought into the room. He wrote a short note, hesitated for nearly twenty minutes, and before he could decide whether to give it to her, she walked over first, simply to ask if he needed anything. That single, unplanned question quietly opened a door that neither of them expected to walk through.

Moments like this often make people wonder whether they are the product of chance or something deeper. In Buddhist thought, this question has a name: duyên — often translated as "fate," though its true meaning is closer to "conditions" or "causes that come together." Buddhism teaches that nothing arises from a single cause alone. A meeting between two people is never the result of one moment, but the quiet accumulation of countless smaller conditions — a decision to visit a particular coffee shop, a habit of arriving at a certain hour, a willingness to write a note despite hesitation. Each condition alone seems insignificant. Together, they form what people later call fate.

This idea, known in Buddhist philosophy as duyên khởi, or dependent origination, does not suggest that events are predetermined. Rather, it teaches that connection is never accidental, yet never guaranteed to last — because the very conditions that brought two people together are also impermanent. This is not a discouraging thought, but an invitation to gratitude. If a bond exists only because of a specific, unrepeatable arrangement of causes, then its presence deserves to be noticed, not taken for granted.

For Alex and Meiu, what looked like a small coincidence in a coffee shop was, in this view, never truly small at all. It was the visible result of many invisible conditions quietly aligning. Buddhist teaching suggests that the proper response to such moments is not possession, but mindfulness — to remain present with the person in front of you, precisely because the conditions that made this moment possible will never arrange themselves in exactly the same way again.
